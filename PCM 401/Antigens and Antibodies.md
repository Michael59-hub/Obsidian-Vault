## Antigens
An antigen is any substance usually a molecule that is recognised by the immune system and can bind to a specific antibody or T cell receptor. Antigenicity is the ability to bind to the final product of an immune response

An immunogen is an antigen that stimulates an immune response. Immunogenicity is the ability to provoke a humoral or cell mediated immune response.

### Determinants of Immunogenicity
#### 1. Foreignness
#### 2. Molecular Weight
<5000 Da are typically weak immunogens. >5000 Da are typically strong immunogens
#### 3. Chemical Complexity and Composition
Complexity: Heteropolymers (good immunogens) vs Homopolymers (bad immunogens) 
Chemical nature: Proteins (best immunogens) vs Polysaccharides (good immunogens) vs Lipids/Nucleic acids (poor immunogens)
#### 4. Physical Form and Degradability
Physical form: Particulate antigens (good immunogens) vs Soluble antigens (bad immunogens)
Degradability: For T cell mediated immune response, the antigen has to be degradable by APCs. Non-degradable antigens(plastic) are poor immunogens.
#### 5. Epitopes
These are the small, specific regions on antigens that act as the binding site to antibodies or T cell receptors. Antigens without epitopes are poor immunogens


## Antibodies
These are Y-shaped glycoproteins produced by plasma cells in response to an antigen.

### Structure
![[chains-structure-antibody-immunoglobulin-molecule-light-unit.webp]]The variable(V) region located at the tips of both arms of the "Y" is the antigen binding site. This region is specific for each antibody and is responsible for recognising and binding to a single specific epitope on an antigen. It contains ==Complementarity-Determining Regions (CDR)==.

The Constant (C) region determines the antibody's isotype and its effector function(e.g. complement activation or binding to immune cells).

FAB fragment (Fragment Antigen Binding) corresponds to both arms of the "Y". It is composed of VL, CL, VH and CH1.
FC fragment (Fragment Crystallisable) corresponds to the stem of the "Y". It mediates effector functions by binding to Fc receptors on immune cells and activating the complement cascade. It is composed of CH2 and CH3(==and CH4 in IgM and IgE==). The hinge region in Ig- A,G and D provides flexibility to the arms of the "Y" allowing them to bind to different spatial arrangement of epitopes


## Classes of Antibodies

| Isotype | Structure                                  | Location and Key Function                                                                                                                                      |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| IgG     | Monomer(Y-shape)                           | Most abundant(80%). Crosses the placenta. Neutralises toxins, opsonisation, complement activation                                                              |
| IgA     | Dimer(In secretions), Monomer (in serum)   | Found in secretions. 13% total antibody content in serum. Has 2 subclasses: IgA1 and IgA2. IgA is called the secretory immunoglobin. Provides mucosal immunity |
| IgM     | Pentamer(five Y units linked by a J chain) | First one produced as infection response. High avidity(10 binding sites). Excellent complement activator and agglutinator. Found in blood and lymph            |
| IgE     | Monomer                                    | Allergic reactions and defence against parasitic worms. Binds to mast cells and basophils to release histamine.                                                |
| IgD     | Monomer                                    | Found on the surface of B cells alongside IgM. It acts as a B cell receptor. Function is still largely unknown                                                 |

### MOA of Antibodies
They don't destroy pathogens. They just **tag them for destruction** or **prevent** them from causing harm. It does so through these mechanisms:
#### 1. Neutralisation
#### 2. Opsonisation
The antibody mainly IgG, coats the pathogen. Phagocytic cells have Fc portion which recognises the fc portion of bound antibodies, promoting/enhancing phagocytosis.
#### 3. Complement Activation
Mainly by IgG and IgM. The lysis is caused by Membrane Attack Complex formed from a complement cascade.
#### 4. Antibody-dependent Cell Mediated Cytotoxicity
Basically opsonisation but this time not phagocytosis; instead it is apoptosis
#### 5. Agglutination
Antigens with high avidity (IgM) can cross link antigens into large, visible clumps. This makes phagocytosis easier and limits their spread.

### Rh Blood Group System
- The antibodies are mainly IgG
- Hemolytic disease of the fetus and new born AKA Erythroblastosis fetalis AKA Rhesus disease
- Erythroblastosis fetalis is now largely preventable through the administration of Rh Immune Globulin(RhIg), commonly known as RhoGAM.
- The injection is administered to the mother at 28 weeks of gestation and again 72 hours post-partum. Also in cases of miscarriage or amniocentesis
- RhoGAM contains exogenous anti-D antibodies. These antibodies will bind to any Rh+ fetal cells and destroy it before it makes contact with the mother's immune system. Therefore, it destroys the antigen before it causes the production of antibodies through the mother's active immunity.

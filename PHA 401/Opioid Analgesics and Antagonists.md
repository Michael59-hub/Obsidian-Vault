

## Classification of Opioids
### Based on Origin
1. Natural: Morphine, Codeine
2. Semi-synthetic: Heroin, Oxycodone
3. Synthetic: Fentanyl, Methadone, Tramadol
### Based on Receptor Activity
1. Full agonists: Morphine, Fentanyl
2. Partial agonists: Buprenorphine
3. Antagonists: Naloxone, Naltrexone

## MOA
They bind opioid receptors present in the body to exert their effects. These receptors include:
1. Mu: Analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, physical dependence, reduced GIT motility
2. Kappa: Spinal analgesia, sedation, miosis, [[Dysphoria]]
3. Delta: Modulates μ receptor activity, Analgesia(spinal), modulation of mood, role in tolerance.
They also inhibit NT release by reducing calcium influx and increasing potassium efflux.
Specifically, they bind to Gi receptors thereby reducing cAMP, opening K+ channels(hyper-polarisation) then closing Ca2+ channels(reduced NT release


## Pharmacological Actions
In the CNS: 
1. Analgesia
2. Sedation, euphoria (μ) or dysphoria (κ)
3. Respiratory depression (dose-dependent)
4. Cough suppression (codeine, dextromethorphan)
5. Miosis ("pinpoint pupils")
6. Nausea/vomiting (stimulates CTZ)
Peripheral:
7. Decrease GI motility → constipation
8. Constriction of biliary tract
9. Release of histamine  leading to vasodilation, pruritus, hypotension


## Therapeutic Uses
1. Pain relief (morphine, fentanyl, oxycodone)
2. Premedication before anesthesia (fentanyl, sufentanil, remifentanil)
3. Cough suppression (codeine, dextromethorphan)
4. Treatment of diarrhoea (Loperamide, Diphenoxylate)
5. Relief of [[Dyspnea]] in pulmonary edema or heart failure.
6. Opioid dependence therapy (methadone, buprenorphine)

## Adverse Effects
1. Respiratory depression
2. Nausea and vomiting
3. Constipation and Urinary retention
4. Tolerance and physical dependence
5. Miosis
6. Withdrawal symptoms after discontinuation

## Opioid Antagonists
1. Naltrexone(Long acting, IV,  used in opioid emergencies)
2. Naloxone(Short acting, Oral, used in maintenance therapy for opioid/alcohol dependence)
3. Nalmefene (Longer acting than Naloxone, used in opioid overdose and dependence)
MOA: Competitive inhibition

## Opioid Partial Agonists
1. Buprenorphine: Partial μ agonist, κ antagonist → used in opioid dependence
2. Pentazocine: κ agonist, weak μ antagonist → less abuse potential but can cause dysphoria
3. Butorphanol, Nalbuphine: Mixed actions; used for moderate pain, **obstetric analgesia**

## Clinical Considerations
1. Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration.
2. Monitor for signs of tolerance and dependence.
3. Avoid in patients with respiratory diseases.
4. Gradual tapering to prevent withdrawal.
5. Patient education on risks of misuse and overdose.

## Notes
- Morphine **triad** in overdose: Coma (**CNS depression**), pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression.

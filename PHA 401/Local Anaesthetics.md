Local anaesthetics are drugs that reversibly block nerve conduction when applied to a specific area of the body producing loss of sensation without loss of consciousness.

## MOA
They block voltage-gated sodium ion channels in excitable membranes reversibly
### Important Notes on the MOA
- Small myelinated fibers are blocked before large unmyelinated fibers.[^1]
- Order of block: APT-TPM(Autonomic -> Pain -> Temperature -> Touch/Pressure -> Proprioception -> Motor).
- After discontinuation, nerve function returns in the reverse order.

## Properties of an Ideal LA
1. Non-irritating
2. Non-allergenic
3. Ease of administration
4. Painless application
5. Rapid onset of action
6. Appropriate duration of action
7. High potency
8. Selective action
9. Low systemic toxicity
10. Wide therapeutic margin
11. Compatibility
12. Minimal vasodilation
13. Reversible effects
14. No residual effects
15. . Chemical stability

## Classification of LAs

| Feature              | Amino Esters                                              | Amino Amides                                                                              |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Duration of action   | Shorter                                                   | Longer                                                                                    |
| Stability            | Relatively unstable                                       | Relatively stable                                                                         |
| Metabolism           | Plasma cholinesterases                                    | Hepatic amidases                                                                          |
| Metabolic by-product | Para-aminobenzoic acid(PABA)                              |                                                                                           |
| Characteristics      | Allergy/Hypersensitivity                                  | Good tissue penetration and low skin corrosion                                            |
| Examples             | Chloroprocaine, Benzocaine, Cocaine, Procaine, Tetracaine | Lidocaine, Bupivacaine, Levobupivacaine, Ropivacaine, Mepivacaine, Prilocaine, Etidocaine |

## Pharmacological Actions
- CNS stimulation at low doses and depression at high doses
- Cardiac depression
- Vasodilation(except cocaine)
- Muscle twitching, transient neurological symptoms[^2]
- Reduction of smooth muscle tone in bronchi, GIT and uterus[^2]
- Reduce Ach release(at high doses)
- Metheglobinemia


## Clinical Uses
- Minor surgical procedures
- Dental procedures
- Regional anesthesia
- Pain management
- Diagnostic procedures


## Side Effects and Complications
- Systemic toxicity
- Allergic reactions(Amino esters - PABA)

## Notes
- They are often combined with epinephrine to prolong duration, reduce systemic absorption, reduce toxicity and improve hemostasis
- LAs should not be used around end arteries since ischemia may result from vasoconstriction 



---


[^1]: This is because myelin acts as an electrical insulator and so the sodium channels are highly concentrated at the nodes of Ranvier. Meaning that a LA agent has to just block a small area to block nerve conduction. Meanwhile, for large unmmyelinated fibers, the LA agent has to cover the entire axon to prevent nerve conduction. This also explains why action potential jumps from one node to another in myelinated fibers.

[^2]: Clinically insignificant at therapuetic doses

These groups of disorders are characterized by progressive and irreversible loss of neurons from specific regions of the brain.

Examples of these neurodegenerative disorders are
- Parkinson's Disease(PD) 
- Huntington's Disease(HD)
- Alzheimer's Disease(AD)
- Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis(ALS)

## Aetiology of Neurodegenerative Disorders
The aetiology of these disorders is grouped into:
### 1. Genetic
Genetic predisposition plays an important role in aetiology. HD is transmitted by Autosomal dominant inheritance. Most cases of the rest occur sporadically.

In PD, mutations in 4 different proteins can lead to genetically determined forms of the disease: α- Synuclein, Parkin, UCHL1, DJ-1.

In AD, the dependent genes are Amyloid Precursor Protein (APP) and presenilins. Apolipoprotein E is also a risk factor.

In ALS, the dependent gene is SOD-1
### 2. Environmental
These environmental factors include: Infectious agents, toxins, and acquired brain injury.

### 3. Intrinsic physiological characteristic of the affected population of neurons.
These characteristics include: susceptibility to excitotoxicity, capacity for oxidative metabolism and aging, capacity to manage oxidative stress



## Parkinson's Disease
It was first described by James Parkinson in 1817 as paralysis Agitans or shaking palsy.

PD is the most common cause of Parkinsonism which is a clinical syndrome characterized by four cardinal motor features namely: Bradykinesia, muscular rigidity, resting tremor, and impairment of postural balance

Note: Parkinsonism is a clinical syndrome characterized by bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor, and postural instability due to dopamine deficiency. It is a family of symptoms with clinical features

The pathological hallmark of PD is loss of pigmented, dopaminergic neurons of the Substantia nigra pars compacta with the appearance of intracellular inclusions known as Lewy bodies.

**Diagnosis**

PD is diagnosed clinically using the signs and symptoms. There are primary criteria and secondary criteria

**1)** Primary criteria include: Bradykinesia + Resting Tremor or Muscular rigidity

**2)** Supportive criteria/ Secondary criteria: Response to levodopa therapy and DaT scan\


### Pathophysiology 
The main deficit/defect in PD is a loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra which provide dopaminergic innervation to the striatum (caudate and putamen). So administration/replacement of dopamine would restore function.

Dopamine has two receptors- D1 and D2. D1(direct pathway)[^1] stimulate the release of cAMP as a second messenger while D2(indirect pathway)[^2] inhibits cAMP, suppresses Ca2+ influx and activate receptor operated K+ ion channel.

All the dopamine receptors are heptahelical G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs). The five dopamine receptors can be divided into two groups on the bases of their pharmacological and physical properties namely

-          D1 & D5 – Have long intracellular carboxy-terminal tail and are members of the class defined pharmacologically as D1

They stimulate formation of cyclic AMP and phosphatidyl – inositol hydrolysis.

-          D2, D3 & D4 – Have a third intracellular loop are of the D2 class they decrease cyclic AMP and modulate Ca2+ and K+ current.

In a healthy brain, the **neostriatum** acts as the entry point for the basal ganglia, receiving commands from the cortex. It uses two pathways to regulate movement: the **direct pathway**, which facilitates movement by disinhibiting the thalamus, and the **indirect pathway**, which suppresses movement.

**Dopamine** is the essential regulator that keeps these two in balance by stimulating the "Go" (direct) route and silencing the "No-Go" (indirect) route. In **Parkinson’s Disease**, the loss of dopamine creates an imbalance where the "brakes" (indirect pathway) are stuck on and the "gas pedal" (direct pathway) fails. This leaves the thalamus suppressed, preventing the cortex from executing smooth, voluntary movements.

## Treatment of Parkinson's
### 1. Levodopa
- Most effective in treating PD
- Absorbed by the small intestine by transporter system for aromatic amino acid
- Competition for absorption sites in the small intestine from dietary amino acid may have a marked effect on absorption of levodopa.
- Very low bioavailability
- Carbidopa and Benserazide are peripherally acting inhibitors of aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase. Inhibition of peripheral decarboxylase activity markedly increase the fraction of levodopa that remain unmetabolized and available to cross the blood brain barrier thus also reduce the incidence of gastrointestinal side effects caused by dopamine
### 2. Dopamine Receptor Antagonist
- They are divided into two generations: Older generation include- bromocriptine and pergolide while Newer generation include- ropinirole and pramipexole.
- Bromocriptine is a strong D2 agonist and partial antagonist of D1 receptor  while pergolide is an agonist at both D1 and D2.
- Ropinirole and Pramipexole have selective activity at D2 class of receptor (especially D2 and D3) with little or no activity at D1 class of receptor.
- The main difference between the newer and older generation is that the newer generation have higher tolerability and faster titration 


### 3. Catechol-O-Methyltransferase Inhibitors
- Examples include Tolcapone and Entacapine.
- Tolcapone has a long duration of action while entacapine has a shorter duration of action

### 4. Selective MAO-B Inhibitors
- Examples include [[Selegiline]] and Rasagiline


### 5. Muscarinic Receptor Antagonist
- There were used prior to the discovery of levodopa. They act on muscarinic receptor on the striatal interneurons.
- Trihexyphenydyl (2-4mg 3 times daily Artane), benztropine mesylate (1-4mg) diphenlydramine hydrochloride.
- They are used in the treatment of early Parkinson’s disease or as adjunct to dopatherapy.
- Their adverse effect result from their anticholinergic activity and include sedation and mental confusion, constipation, urinary infection, blurred vision as a result of cycloplegia.


### 6. Amantidine
- Antiviral for the prophylaxis and treatment of Influenza A
- It alter dopamine release in the striatum and possess anticholinergic properties
- Its ability to block NMDA glutamate receptor may be responsible for its significant antiparkinson activity
- Amantadine is used as initial therapy in mild PD. And as adjunct therapy in patient with dose related flunctuations and dyskinesia. The antidyskinesic property of Amantadine has been attributed to its effect on NMDA receptor.
- Side effects include lethargy, dizziness, anticholinergic effect, and sleep disturbance nausea and vomiting.

### 7. Neuroprotective Agents
These agents are thought to modify the progressive neurodegeneration that underlies PD rather than simply controlling the symptoms as with many other antiparkinson agents.

The use of dopamine receptor agonist with resultant decrease in generation of dopamine metabolite implicated in the degenerative process is based on this observation.

Also the use of compounds that augment cellular energy metabolism such as coenzyme Q10 which is a cofactor required for the mitochondrial electron transport chain.



## Alzheimer's Disease
This is a clinical condition characterized by a marked atrophy of the cerebral cortex and loss of cortical and subcortical neurons.

This disease result in impairment of cognitive abilities that is gradual in onset but relentless in progression. Impairment of short-term memory is the first clinical feature while the retrieval of distant information is reserved.

The ability to calculate exercise visuospatial skills and use common objects and tools (ideomotorapraxia) are also lost as the disease progresses. At advanced stage of the disease the level of awareness or alertness is also affected there is also muscular contracture but no motor weakness.

Death often results from a complication of immobility such as pulmonary embolism or pneumonia and occurs around 6 to 12 years of onset of the disease.

The diagnosis of AD is based on clinical features supported by laboratory test.

### Pathophysiology
Neurochemical disturbance in AD include reduction in certain cortical neurotransmitters especially acetylcholine and this parallel the loss of cholinergic neuron.

There is progressive atrophy and loss of subcortical cholinergic neurons particularly those in the basal forebrain (nucleus basalis of Meynert) that provides cholinergic innervate to the entire cerebral cortex.

The pathological hallmark of AD is the loss of cortical and subcortical neurons, accumulation of B-amyloid plagues and formation of Neurofibrillar tangles.

The semile plagues and neurofibrillary tangles are abundant in the hippocampus and cortex while the visual and motor articles are spared. This corresponds to the marked memory impairment.

## Pharmacotherapy
1. Use of Ach precursors such as choline chloride and phosphatidyl choline(lecithin).
2. Use of AChE inhibitors such as Physiostigmine, Rivastigmine, Galantamine, Tacrine etc.
3. NMDA antagonists such as Memantine.

Diabetes mellitus is a group of chronic metabolic disorders characterised by high blood glucose levels(hyperglycemia) caused by a deficiency in insulin secretion or insulin action or both.

## Types of Diabetes Mellitus
1. Type I Diabetes Mellitus: Diabetes mellitus caused by a reduction in insulin secretion which in turn is caused by the autoimmune destruction of Beta cells in the pancreas. More common in children and adolescents.
2. Type II Diabetes Mellitus: Diabetes mellitus caused by insulin resistance by the muscles, adipose tissue etc. Most common form of diabetes. Higher incidence in adults.
3. Gestational Diabetes Mellitus: Diabetes mellitus caused by pregnancy-related insulin resistance. Resolves after delivery but with increased risk of type II.
4. Others: Diabetes due to pancreatic disease and then genetic forms of diabetes.

## Clinical Manifestations of DM
•Frequent urination (polyuria)
•Excessive thirst (polydipsia)
•Increased hunger (polyphagia)
•Unexplained weight loss (especially in type 1)
•Fatigue and weakness
•Blurred vision
•Slow healing of wounds
•Recurrent infections
•Type 1 diabetes often presents suddenly, while type 2 diabetes may develop gradually and remain unnoticed for years.

## Complications
### Acute
- Hypoglycemia
- Diabetic Ketoacidosis- More common in Type I
- Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state
### Chronic
#### Microvascular
- Diabetic retinopathy
- Diabetic nephropathy
- Diabetic neuropathy
#### Macrovascular
- Coronary heart disease
- Stroke
- Peripheral vascular disease
## Pharmacological Treatment
### Type I
- Insulin therapy
### Type II
- [[Metformin]]- First line drug
- Sulfonylureas- Increases insulin secretion
- [[DPP-4 Inhibitors]]- Promote insulin response
- GLP-1 Receptor Agonists- enhance insulin release and support weight control
- SGLT2 Inhibitors- Increase glucose excretion in the urine
- Insulin- used when oral medications are insufficient
### Gestational Diabetes
- Managed with exercise and dieting
- Insulin is used if glucose levels spiral out of control


## Classification of Insulin Preparations
1. Rapid-acting Insulin: Onset-10 to 30 mins, Duration- 3 to 5hrs. Examples are insulin lispro, aspart, glulisine. It is used before meals to control ==postprandial glucose==.
2. Short-acting Insulin: Onset-30 to 60mins, Duration- 6 to 8 hrs. Can be given subcutaneously  or through IV. Examples: regular insulin.
3. Intermediate-acting Insulin: Onset- 1 to 2hrs, duration- 12 to 18hrs. Used for ==basal glucose control==. Examples include NPH insulin.
4. Long-acting Insulin: Onset-1 to 2hrs, duration-24 to 42hrs. Provides ==steady basal insulin levels==. Examples include Insulin glargine, detemir, degludec.


## Insulin MOA
Insulin binds to its receptor which is a transmembrane protein receptor with 2 subunits(alpha and beta). It binds the alpha subunit outside the cell which causes  conformational change which makes the beta subunit autophosphorylate. The activated receptor then further phosphorylates proteins called Insulin Receptor Substrate (IRS) proteins. These proteins then go on to activate different signalling pathways such as PI3K-Akt pathway(metabolic effects) and MAP kinase pathway (growth and gene expression effects).
## Drug Interactions
1. Beta blockers and alcohol increase insulin's effect.
2. Corticosteroids and thiazide diuretics decrease insulin effect.
3. Beta-blockers may mask symptoms of hypoglycemia.
## Insulin Administration
•Given via subcutaneous injection using syringes, pens, or pumps
•Common sites: abdomen, thigh, upper arm
•Rotation of injection sites is important to prevent lipodystrophy


## Pharmacology of Non-Insulin Antidiabetics
They are used mainly in type II. They are classified as follows:
### 1.Biguanides
#### Example
- Metformin
#### Mechanism of Action
- Decreases hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis)
- Increases insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues
- Enhances glucose uptake by muscle
- Reduces intestinal glucose absorption
#### Pharmacological Effects
- Lowers fasting and ==postprandial== blood glucose
- Does not cause hypoglycemia
- May promote modest weight loss
#### Adverse Effects
- Gastrointestinal upset (nausea, diarrhea)
- Rare: lactic acidosis
#### Clinical Use
- ==First-line== drug for Type 2 diabetes

### 2.Sulfonylureas
#### Examples
- Glibenclamide (glyburide)
- Glipizide
- Gliclazide
#### Mechanism of Action
- Stimulate insulin release from pancreatic beta cells
- Close ATP-sensitive potassium channels
- Cause cell depolarization and insulin secretion
#### Pharmacological Effects
- Reduce blood glucose by increasing insulin levels
#### Adverse Effects
- Hypoglycemia
- Weight gain
#### Clinical Use
- Used when insulin secretion is still present
### 3.Meglitinides
#### Examples
- Repaglinide
- Nateglinide
#### Mechanism of Action
- Similar to sulfonylureas but with shorter duration
- Stimulate rapid insulin release after meals
#### Advantages
- Better control of post-meal glucose spikes
- Lower risk of prolonged hypoglycemia

### 4.Thiazolidinediones
##### Examples
- Pioglitazone
- Rosiglitazone
#### Mechanism of Action
- Activate PPAR-γ nuclear receptors
- Increase insulin sensitivity in adipose tissue and muscle
- Reduce insulin resistance
#### Adverse Effects
- Weight gain
- Fluid retention
- Increased risk of heart failure
#### Clinical Use
- Used in insulin-resistant patients

### 5.Alpha-glucosidase inhibitors
#### Examples  
- Acarbose
- Miglitol
#### Mechanism of Action
- Inhibit intestinal enzymes that break down carbohydrates
- Delay glucose absorption from the gut
#### Pharmacological Effects
- Reduce postprandial hyperglycemia
#### Adverse Effects
- Flatulence
- Abdominal discomfort
- Diarrhea

### 6.DPP-4 inhibitors
#### Examples
- Sitagliptin
- Saxagliptin
- Linagliptin
#### Mechanism of Action
- Inhibit DPP-4 enzyme
- Prolong action of incretin hormones (GLP-1)
- Increase insulin release and decrease glucagon
#### Advantages
- Weight neutral
- Low risk of hypoglycemia

### 7.GLP-1 receptor agonists
#### Examples
- Exenatide
- Liraglutide
- Semaglutide
#### Mechanism of Action
- Mimic incretin hormone GLP-1
- Increase insulin secretion
- Suppress glucagon
- Slow gastric emptying
#### Benefits
- Weight loss
- Cardiovascular benefits
#### Adverse Effects
- Nausea
- Vomiting

### 8.SGLT-2 inhibitors
#### Examples
- Canagliflozin
- Dapagliflozin
- Empagliflozin
#### Mechanism of Action
- Inhibit glucose re-absorption in renal tubules
- Increase glucose excretion in urine
#### Advantages
- Weight loss
- Reduced blood pressure
- Cardiovascular and renal protection
#### Adverse Effects
- Urinary tract infections
- Genital infections
- Dehydration

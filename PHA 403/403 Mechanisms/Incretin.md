**Incretins** are **gastrointestinal hormones** released after oral glucose intake that **enhance insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner**.

➡️ They explain why **oral glucose causes more insulin release than IV glucose** (the _incretin effect_).

---

### **Main incretin hormones**

1. **GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1)**
    
    - Secreted by **L cells** of distal ileum and colon
        
2. **GIP (Glucose-dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide)**
    
    - Secreted by **K cells** of duodenum and jejunum
        

---

### **Physiological actions of incretins**

In the presence of elevated blood glucose, incretins:

- ↑ **Insulin secretion** from β-cells
    
- ↓ **Glucagon secretion** from α-cells
    
- ↓ **Gastric emptying** (GLP-1 mainly)
    
- ↑ **Satiety** (GLP-1 → appetite suppression)
    

➡️ Net effect: **Lower post-prandial blood glucose**

---

### **Incretin effect (exam favourite)**

> Oral glucose → **greater insulin response** than intravenous glucose at the same plasma glucose level.

Reason: **release of incretins from the gut**.

---

### **Inactivation**

- Incretins are rapidly degraded by **DPP-4 enzyme**
    
- Half-life: **~1–2 minutes**
    

---

### **Clinical relevance**

- **DPP-4 inhibitors** → prevent incretin breakdown
    
- **GLP-1 receptor agonists** → mimic incretin action
    
- In type 2 diabetes → **reduced incretin effect**
    

---

### **Key exam pearls**

- Act only when glucose is high → **no hypoglycaemia**
    
- Released from gut after meals
    
- Short half-life due to DPP-4

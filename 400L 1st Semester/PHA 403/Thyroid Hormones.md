## Thyroid Hormone Synthesis
1. Iodide uptake via NIS(sodium-iodide symporter) into the thyroid follicular cells. ==Inhibited by thiocyanates and perchlorates==.
2. Iodide moves from the follicular cell into the follicular lumen(colloid)
3. In the colloid, iodide is oxidised to iodine by Thyroid Peroxidase(TPO). This requires hydrogen peroxide. ==Blocked my methimazole and propylthiouracil==.
4. Organification(Iodination of Tyrosine). Iodine binds to tyrosine residues on thyroglobulins. Mediated by TPO. It produces 2 forms: monoiodothyronine(MIT) and diiodothyronine(DIT).
5. Coupling of DIT to DIT produces T4(thyroxine) while DIT to MIT produces T3(triiodothyronine). T4 is produced in larger amounts but T3 is the active form.
## Storage
T₃ and T₄ remain attached to thyroglobulin and are stored in the **colloid**.
## Release
- TSH stimulates endocytosis of colloid into follicular cells.
- Thyroglobulin is degraded by **lysosomal enzymes**.
- Free **T₃ and T₄** are released into the bloodstream.
## Peripheral Conversion
Organs convert T4 to T3 for use. This process if catalysed by ==5-deiodinase==. This process is inhibited by PTU, propranolol, glucocorticoids etc.

## Physiological Function of T3/T4
- ↑ Basal metabolic rate
- Growth & CNS development
- ↑ Cardiac output (HR + contractility)
- ↑ GI motility
- Potentiates catecholamines
- Regulates lipid & carbohydrate metabolism

## Hyperthyroidism
Basically excess T3 and T4 production. Most common cause is ==Grave's disease==. Other causes include: Toxic adenoma, toxic multinodular goiter, thyroiditis. It can lead to ==thyroid storm==.
### Beta blockers in Hyperthyroidism management
The use of beta blockers in the management of hyperthyroidism is symptomatic. Meaning that beta blockers will not treat the root cause but will help in the management of disease by alleviating symptoms.
1. It reduces palpitations and tachycardia caused by the positive ionotropic and chronotropic effect of the thyroid hormones on the heart by blocking the binding of epinephrine/norepinephrine.
2. Hyperthyroidism causes an overexpression of beta blockers on the bones which leads to tremors. Beta blockers will bind to these receptors, make them 
3. Beta blockers like propranolol also inhibit the conversion of T4(inactive) to T3(active).

## Grave's Disease
This is an autoimmune disorder caused by thyroid-stimulating immunoglobin(also called **TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb)**) that bind to and activate the TSH receptor which causes increased synthesis and release of T3/T4.
Features: diffuse goiter, exophthalmos, pretibial myxedema
## Hypothyroidism
Deficient thyroid hormone production. Most common cause is Hashimoto's thyroiditis

## Hashimoto's Syndrome
A **T-cell–mediated autoimmune destruction** of the thyroid gland, leading to **progressive hypothyroidism**, often with an initial transient hyperthyroid phase (**hashitoxicosis**). The antibodies involved are Anti-Thyroid Peroxidase (Anti-TPO) and Anti-thyroglobulin antibodies.

## Anti-thyroid Drugs
### 1. Thioamides
#### Examples
- Methemazole
- Carbimazole
- Propylthiouracil
#### MOA
Inhibits Thyroid peroxidase and so inhibits synthesis. They also block peripheral T4 -> T3 conversion(propylthiouracil).
### Notes
- Can cause rash, GI upset, agranulocytosis, hepatoxicity
### 2. Iodides
#### MOA
Inhibits hormone release through the wolff-chaikoff effect
#### Notes
- Metallic taste, salivary swelling
- Hypothyroidism
### 3. I-131
#### MOA
Destroys thyroid follicular cells
#### Notes
- Permanent hypothyroidism
### 4. Propranolol
#### MOA
Reduces symptoms and reduces T4 -> T3

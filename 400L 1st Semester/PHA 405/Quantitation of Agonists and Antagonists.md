Quantitation in pharmacology refers to the measurement and comparison of drug effects. Agonists and antagonists are quantified mainly by studying their dose–response relationships using experimental preparations.
## Quantitation of Agonists
### 1. Dose-Response Curve
This is the most important method of quantifying agonists. Types of dose-response curve:
- Graded dose-response drug: Response increases gradually with dose. It shows the response of a **single tissue or organism**.
- Quantal dose-response drug: This exhibits an all or none effect. It shows response in a population.
#### Parameters used in Agonist Quantitation in DR
1. Maximum Effect(Emax): Highest response an agonist can produce. It measures efficacy.
2. Effective Concentration(EC50): Dose or concentration that produces 50% of maximal response. Measures potency.
3. Potency: The amount of drug needed to produce a given effect. Compared by the position of DR curve.
4. Efficacy: Ability of a drug to produce a maximal response. It depends on intrinsic activity

### 2. Bioassay Methods
Used when chemical methods are difficult
- Matching method: Test dose of agonist is matched with a standard dose producing the same effect.
- Bracketing Method: Effect of test drug is bracketed between two known doses of a standard agonist.
- Interpolation Method: Response of test drug is interpolated on the standard dose–response curve.
- Three/Four Point Assay: Compares responses of test and standard agonists at fixed dose points. Highly accurate and commonly used in labs 
### 3. Graded vs Quantal Response Analysis
- Graded response: measures intensity of effect in an individual (e.g., muscle contraction)
- Quantal response: measures all-or-none responses in a population (==used to calculate ED₅₀==)
### 4. Receptor Binding Assay(Indirect)
- Measure agonist affinity for receptors (Kd, Ki). Does not measure efficacy directly
### 5. Intrinsic Activity(alpha-value)
Compares agonist effect to a full agonist. Full agonist: α = 1, Partial agonist: α < 1.

## Quantitation of Antagonists
### Methods for Competitive Antagonists
#### 1. Effect of Antagonists on DR Curves
Antagonists are quantified by observing ==how they shift== the agonist dose–response curve. The degree of shift reflects agonist potency in competitive antagonism.
#### 2. Dose Ratio
$$
DR= (EC50~in~presence~of~antagonist)/(EC50~without~antagonist)
$$
#### 3. Schild Plot (Schild Regression Analysis)
This is a graphical method for quantifying competitive antagonists.
•A graphical method for quantifying competitive antagonists
•Plots log (dose ratio – 1) against log antagonist concentration
•Used to:
–Confirm competitive antagonism
–Calculate pA₂ value
•A straight line with slope ≈ 1 indicates competitive antagonism
•Most important and precise method.
•Steps:
•Plot log(DR – 1) on Y-axis
•Against log antagonist concentration on X-axis
•Interpretation:
•Slope ≈ 1 → competitive antagonism
•pA₂ value = –log antagonist concentration that requires doubling of agonist dose
•📌 pA₂ is the quantitative measure of competitive antagonist potency

#### 4. pA2 Value
- pA₂ is the negative logarithm of the antagonist concentration that requires doubling the agonist dose to produce the same response
- Higher pA₂ value → more potent antagonist
- Independent of agonist concentration
#### 5. Gaddum Equation(Theoretical method)
$$
DR= 1+ (B/KB)
$$Where B= antagonist concentration and KB= dissociation constant of antagonist. 
Used mainly for theoretical and research purposes.

### Methods for Non-Competitive Antagonists
These are quantified by the degree of reduction in Emax.
#### 1. Reduction in Maximal Response(Emax Method)
- The agonist dose–response curve is recorded in the absence and presence of increasing concentrations of the non-competitive antagonist.
- The antagonist causes a progressive reduction in the maximal response.
- Primary method for identifying and quantifying non-competitive antagonism
- The degree of Emax reduction reflects antagonist strength
#### 2. DR Curve Analysis
- Plot graded dose–response curves of the agonist with different fixed concentrations of the antagonist. So the concentration of the agonist increases in each graph but the antagonist is fixed in that particular graph.
- Characteristic changes include: Curves are non-parallel, ceiling effect is reduced, downward shift of the curve.
- Confirms non-competitive antagonism
- Allows comparison of antagonist potency based on degree of curve depression
#### 3. IC50 Method
- IC50 is the concentration of antagonist that produces 50% of inhibition of agonist's maximal response.
- Widely used in experimental and receptor studies
- Useful for comparing the potency of different non-competitive antagonists
- Depends on agonist concentration and experimental conditions
#### 4. Apparent Receptor Inactivation Method
- Common in irreversible antagonist studies
- Non-competitive antagonists effectively reduce the number of functional receptors.
- The reduction in response reflects the proportion of receptors inactivated.

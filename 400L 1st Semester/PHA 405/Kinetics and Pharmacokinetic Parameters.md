## Efficacy
Asymptotic maximal response
## Intrinsic Efficacy
Power of a drug to induce a response
## Threshold Dose
This is the dose that produces a just noticeable effect
## Ceiling Dose
Lowest does that produces a maximal effect
## Inert Binding Site
Binding of a drug to a non regulatory molecule such as plasma proteins will result in no detectable change in the function of the biologic system, so this endogenous molecule can be called an inert
binding site.
## Clearance
Measure of the ability of the body to eliminate a drug. It is the factor that predicts the rate of elimination in relation to the drug concentration. Amount of drug cleared from the body per unit time.
CL=(Rate of elimination)/C(drug concentration)
CL = Dose/AUC
Cl = (0.7 x Vd)/T-half
- Clearance is the single most important factor determining drug concentrations.
- The factors that influence clearance include dose, the organ blood flow, and the intrinsic function of the liver or kidneys.
### Types of Elimination
#### 1. First order elimination
- Rate of elimination is directly varies with drug concentration. 
- Clearance is then estimated by calculating the area under the curve of the time-concentration profile after dose.
#### 2. Capacity-limited Elimination
- Also known as mixed-order, saturable, dose- or concentration-dependent, nonlinear, and Michaelis Menten elimination.
- Clearance varies depending on concentration of drug that is achieved
- This type of elimination occurs as a result of elimination pathways becoming saturated when the dose/concentration are high enough.
- Rate of elimination =("Vmax(maximum elimination capacity)"  × C)/C. 
- Drugs that show this type include: aspirin, phenytoin and ethanol
#### 3. Flow-dependent Elimination
- Drug elimination directly varies with drug delivery(blood flow)
- Drugs that undergo this are called ==high extraction drugs==
- Drugs are cleared on first pass
- Drugs that show this type include: [[Desipramine]], [[Imipramine]], [[Isoniazid]], Labetalol


## Volume of Distribution
The measure of the apparent space in the body available to contain the drug.
Vol of Dist=(Amount of drug in the body)/Concentration of drug in (blood,  plasma or water)
## Steady state
This is the state at which the rate of drug administration(dosage) equals the rate of drug elimination.

## Half Life
$T-half = (0.693 * Vol of distribution)/CL$
- This is the time required to reduce the amount of drug in the body by one-half during elimination
- Half life indicates the time it takes to attain 50% of steady state or to decay 50% from steady state conditions after a change in rate of drug administration

## Maintenance Dose
- This is the dose of drug required to maintain a steady-state of drug in the body
- More related to clearance
$$
 Maintenance.dosing.rate = Clearance * Target Concentration  
$$
$$
Dosing Rate Oral=  (Dosing Rate)/Foral(Bioavailability)
$$
- If intermittent doses are given:
$$
Maintenance Dose=Dosing Rate ×Dosing Interval
$$
- The main formula for maintenance dose:
$$
Maintenance~Dose= (Dosing~Rate/F)*Dosing~Interval
$$
## Loading Dose
This is the dose required to quickly raise the concentration of drug in plasma to the target concentration(TC).
The volume of distribution is the proportionality factor that relates the total amount of drug in the body to the concentration.
$$
Loading~Dose= Vd*TC
$$
To match peak steady state concentration:
$$
Loading~Dose= Maintenance~Dose*Accumulation~Factor
$$


## Drug Metabolism
Kinetics of Metabolism:
### 1. First-order Kinetics
- They obey Michaelis-Menten kinetics
$$ rate~of~drug~metabolism=(Vmax [C]) /(Km) $$

### 2. Zero-order Kinetics
- Drugs such as aspirin, ethanol , and phenytoin follow this
- Also called non-linear kinetics
$$ rate~of~drug~metabolism=(Vmax [C]) /[C] $$

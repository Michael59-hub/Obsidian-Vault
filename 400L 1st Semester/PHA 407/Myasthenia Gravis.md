## Definition

- Chronic **autoimmune disorder** affecting the [[Neuromuscular Junction]]
    
- Characterized by:
    
    - Fluctuating skeletal muscle weakness
        
    - Fatigability (worse with use, better with rest)
        
- Caused by antibodies against postsynaptic membrane proteins
    

---

## Epidemiology

- Bimodal distribution:
    
    - Young women (20–40 yrs)
        
    - Older men (>50 yrs)
        
- Associated with [[Thymic abnormalities]]
    

---

## Pathophysiology

### Normal NMJ Physiology

- Motor neuron releases [[Acetylcholine (ACh)]]
    
- ACh binds [[Nicotinic ACh Receptors (Nm)]]
    
- Opens Na⁺ channels → depolarization → muscle contraction
    

### In Myasthenia Gravis

Autoantibodies target:

1. **ACh Receptors (~85%)**
    
2. **MuSK (Muscle-Specific Kinase)**
    
3. LRP4 (rare)
    

### Mechanisms of Weakness

- Complement-mediated destruction of postsynaptic membrane
    
- Receptor internalization
    
- Reduced number of functional ACh receptors
    
- Flattening of junctional folds
    

→ ↓ End-plate potential  
→ Failure to reach threshold  
→ Muscle weakness

- Dysarthria(motor speech disorder)
    
- Dysphagia(difficulty swallowing)
    
- Nasal speech
    

### Generalized

- Proximal limb weakness
    
- Neck weakness ("head drop")
    

### Muscles Spared

- Pupillary muscles (important distinction from [[Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome]])
    

---

## Myasthenic Crisis

- Severe respiratory muscle weakness
    
- May require ventilation
    
- Triggers:
    
    - Infection
        
    - Surgery
        
    - Certain drugs (aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers)
        

---

## Diagnosis

### Clinical

- Fatigability pattern
    
- Ice pack test (improves ptosis)
    

### Serology

- Anti-AChR antibodies
    
- Anti-MuSK antibodies
    

### Electrophysiology

- Repetitive nerve stimulation → decremental response
    
- Single-fiber EMG → increased jitter
    

### Imaging

- CT/MRI chest → evaluate thymus
    

---

## Thymus & MG

- Thymic hyperplasia (~70%)
    
- Thymoma (~10–15%)
    

→ Indicates role of thymus in loss of self-tolerance

---

## Treatment

### 1. Symptomatic

**Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors**

- Pyridostigmine
    
- ↑ ACh availability
    

---

### 2. Immunosuppressive Therapy

- Corticosteroids
    
- Azathioprine
    
- Mycophenolate
    
- Rituximab (refractory cases)
    

---

### 3. Rapid Immunomodulation (Acute)

- IVIG
    
- Plasmapheresis
    

---

### 4. Thymectomy

Indicated in:

- Thymoma
    
- Generalized MG (especially anti-AChR positive)
    

---

## Differential Diagnosis

- [[Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome]]
    
- [[Botulism]]
    
- [[Motor Neuron Disease]]
    
- [[Multiple Sclerosis]]

|Feature|Myasthenia Gravis|Lambert-Eaton|

|Antibody target|ACh receptor|Presynaptic Ca²⁺ channels|
|Weakness|Worse with use|Improves with use|
|Reflexes|Normal|Reduced|
|Pupils|Normal|May be affected|
|Malignancy link|Thymoma|Small cell lung carcinoma|
## Core Pathophysiology (Drug-Relevant View)

- Autoimmune destruction of **postsynaptic nicotinic (Nm) acetylcholine receptors**
    
- ↓ Functional ACh receptors
    
- ↓ End-plate potential amplitude
    
- Failure to reach threshold with repeated stimulation
    
- Weakness worsens with activity
    

**Pharmacologic implication:**  
→ Problem is _not_ insufficient ACh release  
→ Problem is insufficient receptor activation  
→ Strategy: ↑ ACh concentration or suppress autoimmunity

---

# Therapeutic Strategy Framework

Treatment targets 3 major domains:

1. **Symptomatic NMJ enhancement**
    
2. **Immune suppression**
    
3. **Rapid antibody removal (crisis management)**
    

---

# 1️⃣ Acetylcholinesterase Inhibitors (First-Line Symptomatic)

## Pyridostigmine (Drug of Choice)

### Mechanism

- Reversible acetylcholinesterase inhibitor
    
- Inhibits breakdown of ACh
    
- ↑ ACh concentration at NMJ
    
- Compensates for ↓ receptor number
    

### Pharmacokinetics

- Oral
    
- Does **not** cross BBB significantly
    
- Duration: ~3–4 hours
    

### Clinical Use

- First-line for mild–moderate MG
    
- Ocular and generalized forms
    

### Adverse Effects (Cholinergic Excess)

Muscarinic:

- Diarrhea
    
- Sweating
    
- Salivation
    
- Bradycardia
    

Nicotinic:

- Muscle fasciculations
    
- Cramps
    

Management of toxicity:

- Atropine (for muscarinic symptoms only)
    

---

## Neostigmine

- Similar mechanism
    
- Shorter acting
    
- More often used in:
    
    - Reversal of non-depolarizing neuromuscular blockers
        

---

## Key Pharmacologic Principle

AChE inhibitors:

- Improve symptoms
    
- Do NOT modify disease progression
    

---

# 2️⃣ Immunosuppressive Therapy (Disease-Modifying)

Used when:

- Inadequate response to pyridostigmine
    
- Generalized disease
    
- Frequent exacerbations
    

---

## Corticosteroids (Prednisone)

### Mechanism

- ↓ T-cell activation
    
- ↓ B-cell antibody production
    
- ↓ anti-AChR antibody levels
    

### Important Clinical Note

- Initial transient worsening may occur
    
- Start low, titrate upward
    

---

## Steroid-Sparing Agents

### Azathioprine

- Inhibits purine synthesis
    
- ↓ lymphocyte proliferation
    
- Slow onset (3–6 months)
    

### Mycophenolate mofetil

- Inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase
    
- Selective for lymphocytes
    
- Used as steroid-sparing
    

### Cyclosporine / Tacrolimus

- Calcineurin inhibitors
    
- ↓ IL-2 transcription
    
- ↓ T-cell activation
    

---

## Rituximab

- Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody
    
- Depletes B cells
    
- Useful in:
    
    - MuSK-positive MG
        
    - Refractory cases
        

---

# 3️⃣ Rapid Immunomodulation (Acute Management)

Used in:

- Myasthenic crisis
    
- Pre-operative optimization
    
- Severe exacerbation
    

---

## IVIG

Mechanisms (multifactorial):

- Saturates Fc receptors
    
- Neutralizes pathogenic antibodies
    
- Modulates complement activation
    

Onset: Days  
Temporary effect

---

## Plasmapheresis

- Direct removal of circulating antibodies
    
- Rapid improvement
    
- Short-lived benefit
    

---

# Myasthenic Crisis vs Cholinergic Crisis (Pharmacology Critical)

## Myasthenic Crisis

Cause:

- Too little effective NMJ transmission
    

Management:

- Increase AChE inhibitors
    
- IVIG / plasmapheresis
    
- Ventilatory support
    

---

## Cholinergic Crisis

Cause:

- Excess ACh (overdose of AChE inhibitors)
    

Features:

- SLUDGE symptoms
    
- Muscle fasciculations
    
- Weakness due to depolarizing block
    

Management:

- Stop AChE inhibitor
    
- Atropine (muscarinic)
    
- Supportive care
    

---

# Drug Interactions That Worsen MG

Mechanisms:  
Either ↓ ACh release or impair NMJ transmission

## Aminoglycosides

- Block presynaptic Ca²⁺ channels
    
- ↓ ACh release
    

## Fluoroquinolones

- Interfere with NMJ transmission
    

## Magnesium

- Inhibits Ca²⁺ entry
    
- ↓ ACh release
    

## Beta-blockers

- May worsen muscle weakness
    

## Non-depolarizing Neuromuscular Blockers

- Increased sensitivity in MG patients
    

---

# Anesthetic Considerations

MG patients:

- Resistant to succinylcholine
    
- Highly sensitive to non-depolarizing blockers
    

Why?

- ↓ ACh receptors → altered response dynamics
    

---

# Thymectomy (Pharmacologic Implication)

- Reduces autoantibody production
    
- May reduce long-term immunosuppressive need
    
- Particularly effective in AChR-positive generalized MG
    

---

# Mechanistic Summary (Exam Gold)

Problem:

- ↓ Postsynaptic receptor density
    

Drugs that help:

- ↑ ACh (pyridostigmine)
    
- ↓ antibody production (steroids, azathioprine)
    
- Remove antibodies (IVIG, plasmapheresis)
    
- Deplete B cells (rituximab)
    

Drugs that worsen:

- ↓ ACh release
    
- Block NMJ
    
- Excess ACh → depolarizing block

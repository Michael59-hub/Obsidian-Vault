Muscle contraction is a highly regulated biological process that converts chemical energy (ATP) into mechanical work.

## Mechanisms of Contraction
### Cardiac & Skeletal Muscle
#### Cognition Zone
When action potential hits a cardiac or skeletal muscle fiber, it leads to the release of Ca2+ from intracellular stores such as the sarcoplasmic reticulum and also the influx of Ca2+ during the plateau phase. Calcium then goes and binds to the Troponin complex. Troponin C then moves Tropomyosin out of the way, exposing myosin binding sites on the actin molecules which allows for their binding. Myosin heads bind to actin and pulls the filaments .ATP hydrolysis then provides the energy needed for fiber shortening which produces contraction. After repolarisation, Ca2+ unbinds the troponin complex

### Smooth Muscles
#### Cognition Zone
When action potential hits a cardiac or skeletal muscle fiber, it leads to the release of Ca2+ from intracellular stores such as the sarcoplasmic reticulum and also the influx of Ca2+ during the plateau phase. Calcium then binds calmodulin which activates myosin light chain kinase to phosphorylate myosin which creates contraction

## Actin
- Globular molecule(G-actin) which polymerises to form filamentous actin(F-actin)
- Constitutes the thin filament of the sacromere.
- Each actin molecule has a binding site for myosin heads.
- Actin filaments are anchored to the Z-line in striated muscle.
- Interaction with myosin generates force and movement.
- Drugs rarely target actin directly due to its ubiquitous presence.
- Experimental toxins (e.g., cytochalasins, phalloidin) affect actin polymerization and are mainly research tools.
- Actin is the monomeric unit of two different cytoskeleton filaments; microfilaments and contractile apparatus.
- Actin filaments in muscles are separated by actin-binding proteins, α-actinin that binds two actin filaments while leaving space for myosin
## Myosin
- Superfamily of motor proteins
- Forms the thick filament of the sacromere
- Myosin heads bind actin and ATP
- Possesses intrinsic ATPase activity.
- Myosin is an ATPase that moves along actin filaments by connecting the hydrolysis of ATP to conformational changes
- Cardiac myosin is a drug target (e.g., **myosin activators** like omecamtiv mecarbil).
- Altered myosin function contributes to cardiomyopathies and muscle weakness disorders.
- Myosin in muscle is Myosin II that is a polymeric molecule with a long rod-like tail domain, assembled into thick bipolar filaments.
## Troponin Complex
- Made up of Troponin T, I & C
- Troponin C binds the calcium ions which initiates the contraction process
- Troponin T binds to tropomyosin which is what blocks the myosin-binding sites on actin
- Troponin I inhibits the interaction between actin and myosin when the muscle is relaxed. It is the part that blocks myosin from binding to actin
- Troponins are biomarkers for myocardial injury(Troponin I and T in the blood)
- Ca²⁺ sensitizers can enhance contractility by modulating troponin function

# Muscle Relaxants
There are classified based on their site of action
## Neuromuscular Blocking Agents
- They block nerve signals to the muscles
### Peripherally Acting Muscle Relaxants
#### Depolarising Muscle Relaxants
- They act like acetylcholine, causing prolonged muscle depolarization and paralysis, but can lead to fasciculations (muscle twitching) initially.
- Examples include Succinylcholine
- Adverse effects include hyperkalemia, hyperthermia
#### Non-depolarising Muscle Relaxants
- Competitive antagonism at nicotinic Ach receptor sites
- Examples include Pancuronium, Vecuronium, Rocuronium, Atracurium etc
### Centrally Acting Muscle Relaxants
- These work in the brain and spinal cord (CNS) to reduce muscle spasms, tension, and tone
- Examples include cyclobenzaprine, methocarbamol, metaxalone, and tizanidine
- Side effects include drowsiness, dizziness, and weakness.
- They interfere with nerve signals in the central nervous system that cause muscles to contract and spasm.
- They act as antispasmodics/spasmolytics
- Examples include phenobarbital, diazepam and tizanidine
- Clinical uses: Multiple sclerosis, Spinal cord injury, Cerebral palsy
### Directly Acting Muscle Relaxants
- These act locally on muscle fibers
- They interfere with calcium release or nerve signals at the NM junction
- They treat conditions such as: cerebral palsy, MS(malignant syndrome) and malignant hyperthermia 
- Examples include ==Dantrolene(Inhibits calcium release from the SR)== and ==Botulinum toxin(blocks NT release at the NM junction)==

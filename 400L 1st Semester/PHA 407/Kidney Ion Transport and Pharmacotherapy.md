## Pharmacotherapy Targeting Renal Ion Transport
### 1. Diuretics
These are drugs that increase urine output by altering ion transport. They inhibit salt and water reabsorption in the nephron. They can cause acute kidney injury by reducing plasma volume and GFR.
#### A. Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors
They block carbonic anhydrase enzymes. Examples include acetazolamide, dorzolamide, brinzolamide, and methazolamide
##### MOA
They block carbonic anhydrase from converting water and CO2 to bicarbonate and protons. This reduces the reabsorption of sodium and bicarbonate, increasing diuresis. In the eye, they reduce aqueous humor production, reducing ocular pressure.
##### Clinical Indications
- Glaucoma
- Acute mountain sickness
- Metabolic alkalosis
##### Adverse Effects
- Metabolic acidosis
- Hypokalemia
- Renal stones
#### B. Loop Diuretics
Examples include furosemide and bumetanide. They act on the thick ascending limb
##### MOA
They inhibit the Na-K-2Cl(NKCC2) cotransporter in the thick ascending loop of the loop of Henle, blocking the reabsorption of sodium, potassium and chloride ions. This keeps the water in the tubule(water follows Na) thus increasing urine output. They bind to the chloride binding site of the NKCC2 cotransporter.
##### Pharmacological Effect
- Increased Na and Cl excretion
- Water retention in the tubule
- Decreased interstitial tonicity in the nephrons
##### Clinical Indications
- Acute pulmonary edema
- Heart failure
- Hypertension
- Hypercalcemia
##### Adverse Effects
- Hypokalemia
- Ototoxicity
- Dehydration
#### C. Thiazide Diuretics
Examples include hydrochlorothiazide. They operate at the distal convoluted tubule
##### MOA
They inhibit the sodium-chloride cotransporter (NCC) in the distal convoluted tubule of the kidney, preventing sodium and water reabsorption leading to diuresis. This also lead to loss of calcium and potassium
##### Pharmacological Effect
- Moderate diuresis
- Increased Ca2+ reabsorption
##### Clinical Indications
- Hypertension
- Nephrolithiasis(calcium stones)
##### Adverse Effects
- Hypokalemia
- Hyperuricemia
- Hyperglycemia
#### D. Potassium Sparing Diuretics
These work in the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct to increase sodium and water retention while reducing potassium loss
##### MOA
1. They block epithelial sodium channels (ENaC) on the cell membrane of principal cells in the collecting duct, reducing sodium entry, which in turn reduces the activity of Na-K-ATPase pump. Less sodium is pumped out so less potassium is pumped out in exchange. E.g. Amiloride and Triamterene.
2. Aldosterone antagonists e.g. Spironolactone and eplerenone. Aldosterone facilitates the production of ENaC channels and ATPase pumps
##### Clinical Indications
- Hyperadrenalism
- Heart failure
##### Adverse Effects
- Hyperkalemia
- Gynecomastia(spironolactone)


### Non-diuretic Drugs Affecting Renal Function
1. NSAIDs. They reduce kidney perfusion
2. Proton pump inhibitors. They cause acute interstitial nephritis
3. Antibiotics. Some are nephrotoxic(aminoglycosides and vancomycin)
4. Contrast agents. They can cause acute kidney injury
5. Laxatives. Some cause kidney damage due to crystal formation
6. SGLT2 inhibitors. Block glucose and Na reabsorption in proximal convoluted tubule.

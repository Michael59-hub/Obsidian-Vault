This is the essential physiological process that links an electrical stimulus(AP) in a muscle cell to  mechanical contraction.
## Fundamental Steps Of EC Coupling
1. Electrical excitation. The action potential causes depolarization of the muscle cell membrane (sarcolemma)
2. Calcium mobilisation. Electric signals trigger a release of Ca2+ from intracellular stores mainly the sarcoplasmic reticulum.
3. Activation of contractile proteins. Ca2+ binds regulatory proteins associated with contraction machinery. Troponin C in cardiac and skeletal muscle while Calmodulin in smooth muscle
4. Contraction. ATP hydrolysis drives the sliding of filaments, shortening the muscle fibers and generating force.
5. Relaxation. Ca2+ is pumped back to the sarcoplasmic reticulum by ==SERCA==(sarcoplasmic/endoplasmic reticulum Ca²⁺ ATPase) in cardiac and skeletal muscle. . In smooth muscle, myosin light chain phosphatase dephosphorylates myosin, allowing relaxation. Tropomyosin re-covers actin binding sites in striated muscle, halting contraction.
## Pharmacological Modulation of EC Coupling
### 1. Calcium Channel Blockers
#### MOA
These inhibit L-type voltage-gated calcium channels in cardiac and smooth muscles. Preventing Ca2+ influx during the plateau phase which reduces intracellular calcium levels. In skeletal muscle, CCBs have minimal effect because skeletal E–C coupling relies mainly on mechanical coupling (DHP-RyR) rather than extracellular Ca²⁺.
#### Pharmacological Effects
- Negative inotropic and chronotropic effect on the cardiac muscles
- Reduced peripheral vascular resistance(vasodilation)
- Minimal effect on skeletal muscles
#### Clinical Applications
- Hypertension, Angina, Certain arrhythmias
### 2. Beta-adrenergic Agonists
#### MOA
Activate β₂-adrenergic receptors on smooth muscle → ↑ cAMP → activation of protein kinase A (PKA). PKA phosphorylates myosin light chain kinase (MLCK), inhibiting its activity → smooth muscle relaxation.
#### Pharmacological Effects
- Bronchodilation
- Vasodilation in skeletal muscle arterioles
- Mild increase in heart rate/contractility
#### Clinical Applications
- Asthma and COPD
### 3. Digoxin Glycosides
#### MOA
Inhibit Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase in cardiac myocytes → ↑ intracellular Na⁺. Increased intracellular Na⁺ reduces Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchange, leading to ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺. More Ca²⁺ is stored in the SR, enhancing Ca²⁺ release during the next action potential → stronger contraction (positive inotropy).
#### Pharmacological Effects
- Increased cardiac contractility
- Slower ventricular rate 
- Reduced automaticity
#### Clinical Applications
- Heart failure, atrial fibrillation/flutter

## Membrane Excitability
This refers to the ability of a cell—especially neurons, skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle, and smooth muscle—to generate and propagate electrical signals in response to stimuli.
- K+ is the primary determinant of Resting Membrane Potential(RMP)
- Na+ depolarises
- Cl- stabilises or slightly hyperpolarises
- Ca2+ very strongly depolarises
- Threshold potential is the critical level of depolarisation needed to open the voltage gated ion channel of a particular ion. It is the minimum membrane potential needed to open an ion channel.
- Graded potentials: Small, local changes; vary with stimulus strength; decremental.
- Action potentials are all or none. There is no reduction in signal

### Modulation of Membrane Excitability
1. Altering of ion gradients
2. Changing channel properties

### Pharmacology of Membrane Excitability
#### 1. Decreasing Excitability
- Local anesthetics (block Na⁺ channels)
- Anticonvulsants (Na⁺ or Ca²⁺ block; ↑ GABA)
- Antiarrhythmics (modulate Na⁺/K⁺/Ca²⁺ channels)
- Muscle relaxants (block NMJ transmission)
#### 2. Increasing Excitability
- AChE inhibitors (in myasthenia gravis)
- Some CNS stimulants (increase neurotransmitter levels)
### Clinical Relevance
Abnormal membrane excitability contributes to: Epilepsy, Arrhythmias, Neuropathic pain, Muscle spasms, Myasthenia gravis, Anesthesia management.

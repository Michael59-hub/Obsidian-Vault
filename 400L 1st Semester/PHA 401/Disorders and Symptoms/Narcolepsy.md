Narcolepsy is a **chronic neurological sleep disorder** where the brain can’t properly regulate **sleep–wake cycles**.

In simple terms: the body **randomly switches between being awake and being in REM sleep**, even when it’s not appropriate.

### Key features

- **Excessive daytime sleepiness** (the big one): overwhelming urge to sleep during the day, even after a “full” night’s sleep
    
- **Sleep attacks**: sudden, irresistible episodes of falling asleep
    
- **Cataplexy** (in some patients): sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by emotions like laughter or excitement
    
- **Sleep paralysis**: temporary inability to move or speak when falling asleep or waking up
    
- **Hypnagogic hallucinations**: vivid, dream-like experiences while awake
    

### Types

1. **Narcolepsy type 1**
    
    - With **cataplexy**
        
    - Usually linked to **loss of hypocretin (orexin)** neurons in the hypothalamus
        
2. **Narcolepsy type 2**
    
    - No cataplexy
        
    - Normal hypocretin levels
        

### Pathophysiology (pharmacy-relevant 👀)

- Caused by **deficiency of hypocretin/orexin**, a neuropeptide that maintains wakefulness
    
- Likely **autoimmune-mediated destruction** of hypocretin-producing neurons
    

### Diagnosis

- **Polysomnography** (overnight sleep study)
    
- **Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT)** → short sleep latency + early REM onset
    

### Treatment (symptomatic, not curative)

- **Excessive sleepiness**: modafinil, armodafinil, amphetamines
    
- **Cataplexy**: sodium oxybate, SSRIs/SNRIs, TCAs

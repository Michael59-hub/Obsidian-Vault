**Infection**: This is the invasion or colonisation of the body by pathogenic microorganisms
**Disease**: This occurs when an infection results in any change from a state of health. It is an abnormal state in which part or all of the body is not capable of carry out its normal function.
**Normal Microbiota**: These are the microbial populations that begins to establish themselves just before birth of the child. These do not produce disease under normal condition
NB: ==A typical human body contains 10^13 body cells while bacteria cells are 10^14==
**Transient Microbiota**: These are microbiota present for several days, weeks or months then disappearing.
**Syndrome**: A specific group of symptoms that always accompanies a disease.
**Communicable disease**: Disease that spread from one host to another whether directly or indirectly
**Non-communicable Disease**: These are caused by microbes that normally inhabit the body and only occasionally cause disease when introduced into the body.

2. They promote the development of the immune system in newborns.

### Harmful Effects
1. Displacement causing infection and harmful effects
2. Decrease in normal microbiota population causing growth of harmful microbes
3. Conversion of normally harmless materials by bacteria into carcinogenic substances. E.g. conversion by bacteria sulfatases of sweeteners to bladder carcinogens(cyclohexamine).
4. Immunosuppression leading to overgrowth and pathogenesis of normal flora

## Koch Postulate/ Germ Theory
- He proved that the pathogen was present in every diseased animal
- Then he took blood samples from infected animals and injected into healthy animals and they developed the disease
- He then cultured the pathogen in fluids outside the animals body and demonstrated that the bacteria could cause anthrax.
- He also did the same for anthrax
The steps for the postulate are:
1. Blood is drawn from an sick animal 
2. A sample of the blood reveals the pathogen
3. The pathogen is then cultivated in a pure culture in the laboratory
4. A sample of the pure culture contaning only one kind of microbe is then injected into a healthy animal. If it displays the same signs and symptoms as a sick animal then evidence exists that this particular disease is caused by this particular pathogen.
### Exemptions to Koch's Postulate
1. Inability to be grown on artificial media e.g. Mycobacterium leprae for leprosy, some viruses
2. Multiple etiology of a single disease
### Alterations to Koch's Postulate
In the cause of inability to grow on artificial media you can inoculate entire tissues into organisms as in pneumonia discovery. 



## Spread of Infection
### 1. Reservoir of Infection
This is a source that continually supplies pathogens in an adequate environment.
### 2. Human Reservoir
People that habor pathogens but show no signs of illness are called carriers. While some other people carry disease at first with no symptoms. Incubation period is the period before symptoms develop
### 3. Animal Reservoir
- Diseases that occur primarily in animals that can be transmitted are Zoonoses.
- Examples of Zoonoses are some type of influenza, rabies, lyme disease, anthrax etc
- There are 150 known Zoonoses.
### 4. Non-living Reservoir
- The two major non-living reservoirs are soil and water.
- Soil contains stuff such as Clostridium botulinum while water contains stuff like Vibrio cholerae

## Transmission of Disease
### 1. Contact Transmission
This is the spread of pathogens by direct(person to person), indirect(reservoir -> formite -> susceptible host) or droplet contact(spread of the common cold). This is the direct transmission of an agent by physical contact between its source and susceptible host. No intermediate object is involved. A non-living organism involved in the spread of disease is termed a ==Formite== e.g. handkerchief, tissue etc. In droplet transmission, they droplets travel only a short distance and so are not termed air borne
### 2. Vehicle Transmission
- Transmission by water(cholera) , food(food poisoning & tape worm ) or air(travels more than 1 metre e.g. measles and tuberculosis)
- Other mediums include blood, IV fluid etc
- Example of disease usually spread by this is Salmonella
### 3. Vector Transmission
- Arthropods are the most important disease vectors. They transmit by mechanical method and biological method.
- Mechanical Transmission is transport of pathogens on the feet of insects or other body parts. E,g, typhoid, dysentery(Shigellosis).
- Biological transmission: Vector gets the pathogen from an individual by biting/sucking, the pathogen proliferates in the vector then it transmits the pathogen to a susceptible host. In some others the pathogens enter the faeces of the vector then the faeces contaminates the food or water of the susceptible host.


## Pattern of Exit
- Pattern of entry is the preferred route by which pathogens enters the body e.g. skin, mucus membrane etc.
- ==Portal of exit== is the definitive route of exit for a pathogen. They are the paths pathogens use to spread basically.
- The most common portals of exit are the respiratory(influenza, pneumoniae) and GI tract(cholera, shigellosis)
- Another route of exit is the urinogenital tract(STDs, typhoid, brucellosis)
- Then lastly we have wounds also infected blood. Basically the same thing
## Nosocomical Infection
- These are also known as hospital acquired infections
- Factors affecting nosocomical infections: **Weakened status of the host**, **pathogens in the hospital environment**, and **chain of transmission in the hospital**
- At a point gram positive Staph aureus was the prime cause of nosocomical infections. The major causes today are gram negatives such as E. coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
- The major bacteria involved are enterobacteria
- Weakened state of host is due to: weakened bronchial, skin or mucus membranes and immunosuppression
- Chain of transmission is otherwise known as the route of transmission
- The principal routes are direct contact and indirect contact.
### Control of Nosocomical Infection
- Frequent hand washing
- Educating personnel
- Disinfection of tubs
- Use of respirators and immunomodifiers
- Minimising the use of immunosuppresants

### Development of Disease
It follows some steps:
#### Period of Incubation
This is the time period between initial infection and first sign/symptom of disease. Virulence= degree of pathogenicity. POI depends on virulence, number of invading microbes, resistance of the host and specific microbe
#### Prodromal Period
Relatively short period that follows POI in some diseases. Characterised by mild symptoms such as general ache and malaise
#### Period of Illness
The disease is most acute here. The person exhibits overt signs and symptoms
#### Period of Decline
Signs and symptoms subside and feeling of malaise diminishes 
#### Period of Covalescence
The person regains strength and the body returns to its pre-diseased state
